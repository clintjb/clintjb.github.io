## Half-Baked Product  
*By weli | Hamburg, rainy Tuesday evening*

You know that moment when you’re elbow-deep in chili fumes at 2 a.m., sweat beading on your forehead, realizing the meat’s been *over the top* too long and now you’ve basically invented edible shoe leather? Yeah. That just happened. Again.  

Turns out, undercooked chili isn’t so different from half-baked products. And let’s be real—we’ve all shipped stuff that felt suspiciously like my failed chili: rushed, underdeveloped, and vaguely hopeful it’ll somehow redeem itself in the slow cooker of user feedback.  

Last week, my son handed me a Lego spaceship that’d lost three thrusters mid-assembly. “Dad, it’s *almost* done!” he announced, grinning like he’d just cracked cold fusion. I almost laughed—until I caught myself staring at our team’s latest dashboard prototype. Same energy. Missing chunks. Held together with duct tape and optimism. We’d spent months polishing the *idea* in PowerPoints while the actual build sat gathering dust like last year’s biltong spices.  

Funny how life mirrors work, isn’t it?  

See, I’ve spent two decades chasing this illusion that “done” means “perfect.” Lean methodology taught me otherwise—*especially* when you’re knee-deep in automotive or aerospace projects where “perfect” is a luxury nobody can afford. Yet somehow, we still treat products like soufflés: if they collapse slightly, we trash the whole thing and pretend it never happened.  

But what if collapse is the point?  

A few years back, I tweaked my jerky recipe after a *disastrous* smoker experiment. Meat turned out tasting like campfire grief. Instead of binning it, I chopped it up, threw it into chili (yes, *that* chili), and called it “rustic.” My wife rolled her eyes—but the kids devoured it. Lesson learned: half-baked isn’t failure. It’s raw material.  

Same goes for products. That flimsy Fortnite API project my son and I hacked together last month? It broke three times before it worked. GitHub Actions kept yelling at us like a frustrated German U-Bahn conductor. But stumbling through it—debugging over lukewarm *Apfelschorle*, explaining rate limits while he tried (and failed) to explain *why* his gamer tag is “SneakyPickle42”—*that’s* where the magic lived. We didn’t need a perfect tool. We needed a messy, functional starting point. Something *real* to iterate on.  

Which brings me back to that dashboard. We finally shipped the half-baked version. No fancy animations. Just a bare-bones thing that *sort of* worked. And guess what? The ops team immediately broke it wider open—found edge cases we’d never considered, suggested tweaks over shared *Currywurst* at lunch. Suddenly, it wasn’t *our* product anymore. It was *theirs*.  

Lean isn’t about minimalism for minimalism’s sake. It’s about respecting the *human* layer between vision and reality. That disconnect? The one where strategies gather dust while frontline teams wrestle with broken tools? Half-baked products bridge it. They force conversations. They admit: *“We don’t have all the answers—but let’s figure it out together.”*  

So maybe stop sweating the shoe-leather moments. That half-baked thing you’re embarrassed to ship? It’s not a failure. It’s your duct tape waiting to happen.  

Now if you’ll excuse me—I’ve got a pot of chili to salvage. Son’s coming home from school soon, and we’ve got a Lego spaceship to finish. Thrusters optional.
